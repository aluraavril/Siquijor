Mystics of Siquijor

The remote island of Siquijor is unique—not just in the Philippines but in all of Southeast Asia—renowned for its history of witchcraft, magic, and folk healing.

Located in the Central Visayas, Siquijor attracts Filipino travelers, including those from abroad, seeking therapies that blend Catholicism (introduced by the Spanish in the 16th century) with shamanic practices like potion-making, exorcism, and herbal fumigation. These natural and supernatural treatments are believed to cure illnesses, which are thought to arise from three major causes.

Black Saturday Rituals

For spiritual power

“Minasa, a waxy black elixir made from over 200 ingredients, is burned during to-ob to dispel spells and spirits. Healers gather its elements over seven Fridays in Lent, cooking it only on Black Saturday, a Philippine day of mourning.”

History of Siquijor

Pre-Colonial Period

Siquijor, a small island in the Central Visayas, is rich in history and legend. Before Spanish colonization, it was called Katugasan, named after the tugas (molave) trees that once covered it.

The Spaniards arrived in 1783, establishing it as a mission station under the Augustinian Recollects. They named it Isla del Fuego (Island of Fire) due to the glow of fireflies in its forests.

Colonial Period

During Spanish and American rule, Siquijor remained isolated, preserving its traditions and folklore. It was once part of Negros Oriental before becoming an independent province in 1971. The island played a role in the Philippine Revolution and World War II, with local guerrillas resisting Japanese forces. Its mysticism, rooted in traditional healing practices, remains a significant part of its cultural identity.

Present

Today, Siquijor is known for its pristine beaches, caves, waterfalls, and historical landmarks. While its tales of magic intrigue visitors, its true charm lies in its warm people, rich traditions, and natural beauty, making it one of the most captivating destinations in the Philippines.
