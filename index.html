Saluwaki in a Bottle

Freshly harvested bottles of sea urchins can be bought at the public market of Siquijor.

Sutukil

Sutukil stands for Sugba (grilled), Tula (stewed), and Kilaw (raw). This method of preparing seafood is a culinary tradition in Siquijor, as well as in other Visayan provinces.

Kinilaw

Kinilaw is the Filipino version of ceviche, made with fresh fish marinated in vinegar, citrus juice, and spices. This dish is light, refreshing, and bursting with flavors.

Balat-Balat or Sea Cucumber

This marine creature is often cooked in soups or stews and is prized for its unique texture and nutritional benefits.

Kinilaw Binisaya nga Kulabutan

A fresh of giant squid eaten raw with vinegar and salt. Another way to cook kulabutan is to saute it (ginisang kulabutan).

Saang sa Alat

Spider conch shells or saang is a popular seafood item in Siquijor. The popular way of cooking it is to boil it in seawater, a recipe they call saang sa alat.

Torta

This sweet, fluffy cake is made with flour, sugar, eggs, and a hint of anise. It’s baked to perfection, resulting in a soft and moist texture.

Tinalay or Pan Bisaya

Tinalay, also known as Pan Bisaya, is a traditional bread in Siquijor. It’s a simple yet flavorful bread made from flour, water, and yeast. The bread is often enjoyed with local spreads or as an accompaniment to meals.

Giant Otap

Giant Otap is a larger version of the traditional otap, a flaky, sugary pastry. This treat is made from puff pastry dough, sugar, and coconut oil, resulting in a crispy and sweet snack.

Kalamay

A sticky, sweet treat made from glutinous rice, coconut milk, and sugar, often wrapped in banana leaves.

Suman

Glutinous rice wrapped in banana leaves, often served with a side of sweetened coconut milk or sugar.

Fruit Wine

Siquijor is known for its fruit wines made from local fruits such as duhat (Java plum), guyabano, mango, and pineapple.
